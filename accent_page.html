    American accent:


    For the American accent, there are several sounds that are different to most English-speaking places.
    For starters, the "R" sound in American is a lot more prominent than in other countries.
    For most places, in words like "are", and "car", the "R" sound isn't really obvious. The words sound more like "ah", and "cah".
    But make sure you don't overdo your "R" sound - don't go "caRRrrRr", go "carr"
    The second sound we are focusing are the T's in the middle of words.
    Words like "little" and "brittle", sound more like "liddle" and "briddle".
    The final sound to learn is the vowel sounds in the words "cot", "caught", "law", "long", etc. In all of these words, the sound is the same.

    Australian accent:

    In Australian accentage, you do not want to pronounce the "r" sounds much if at all.
    For example, r's at the end of words become ah's. So "river" would become "riv-ah". "Car" would become "cah".
    If a word ends with "-ing", cut off the "g", so it becomes "-in". For example, "running" would become "runnin".
    This tip is for sentences in general - at the end of each sentence, raise the tone of your voice in an upward inflection.
    When pronouncing the vowel "a" as in "way", the vowel sound becomes more of an "ai" sound. Remember, don't overdo this sound, or you'll sound ridiculous. "no wai mate!"
    Another "a" sound to work on, such as in the word "ant", "man", or "camera". This vowel becomes more of an "e" as in egg. "Thets a great cemera!"
